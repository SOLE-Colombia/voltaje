  4. Relationship with nature (traditional knowledge and environmental awareness)

Group 4

Autores:

David Linares Luis Ortiz Antonio Diaz Gabriela Leaño Herib Campos

What does water mean to you and your community?

We are going to work with two tools: participatory mapping and ethnographic video.

Participatory mapping:

Participatory mapping will help visualize and understand the social, emotional, and caregiving dynamics surrounding the meaning of water for the community. It is a tool for working with the community through speaking circles.

1. Community Activation and Trust

  • Meeting with community leaders and key community members.
  • Explain the purpose and request permission to document ethically and with consent.

“Supporting Tool: Speaking Circle + Audiovisual Consent Letter.”

Suggested Games and Activities:

1. The Voice of the River (creative presentation activity)

Objective: Break the ice, introduce yourself, and connect with the water.

How to play:

  • Each person introduces themselves by saying their name and completing this sentence:

“If I were water, I would be…” (a waterfall, a calm river, a storm, a cheerful little puddle, etc.)

Variant: Have them imitate the sound or movement of the water they chose.

2. Emotional Water Map (on the floor)

Objective: Identify emotional bonds.

How to play:

  • Draw a large circle on the floor (with tape or branches) as a territory.
  • The facilitator asks questions like:
  • “Where did you feel joy with water?”
  • “Where did you feel afraid of water?”
  • “Where did you learn something important about water?”
  • People walk and place themselves within the map based on their answer. They can share if they wish.

Materials: dried leaves, ribbons, chalk, stones to mark areas.

2. Ask, Feel, and Draw (Participatory Mapping)

Activity:

  • In groups, give each other a large piece of poster board with an outline of the territory (it can be free, not geographic).
  • Ask the question:

What does water mean to you?

Where is water present in your daily life?

Where does water flow? Where does it stop? Where is it lacking?

Draw and write:

  • Rivers, streams, wells, sacred sites, areas of scarcity, memories, uses, threats.
  • You can also use symbols, words, or emotional colors.

Materials: Poster board, markers, crayons, stickers, printed icons, colored paper.

Ethnographic Video

Ethnographic video will help us identify and externally record the underlying dynamics of behavior and meaning with water.

During the mapping:

  • Film the conversations while they draw.
  • Interview key people with open-ended questions (children, scholars, fishermen, women leaders):
  • What memories do you have of water?
  • What rituals or stories exist about water?
  • What has changed in the water in the area?

Also:

  • Record landscapes, bodies of water, environmental sounds, daily activities (washing, sailing, harvesting).

Recommendation: Use close-ups of hands drawing, faces speaking, and the growing line. This adds a lot of visual and emotional value.
